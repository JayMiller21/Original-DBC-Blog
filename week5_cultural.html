Peer Pairing and Feedback at Dev Bootcamp

Here at Dev Bootcamp, the curriculum places a significant focus on constructive teamwork and feedback. This involves regular pairing sessions between students. In these sessions, students work together to solve a challenge, and then provide feedback to one another about their respective teamwork and technical skills.

Pairing with others to solve challenges is not something I would have chosen to do on my own. I generally find it helpful to consult others when I encounter a particular difficulty in solving a problem. And likewise, I'm happy to help others with specific questions or difficulties. However, I generally like to work through problems on my own up until I reach that point. This seems natural to me - why have a partner work with me on the parts I can accomplish on my own? Additionally, I observed during college that I thought more slowly than a lot of people I attemped to solve engineering problems with. This repeated experience made me insecure about how I think and learn. To this day, I feel noticably anxious at the thought of someone observing my problem solving process. I also worry about my ability to keep pace with another individual so that I can equally contribute in a partnership.

All that being said, I have been pleasantly surprised by the value I have given and received in most of my pairing sessions so far. The main benefit of a partnership is that everyone thinks differently. Even given a challenge I am able to complete alone, my parner may express his understanding of a concept in a way that broadens or augments my own understanding. Then, when tougher material comes along, our varied perspectives give us more collective wisdom with which to understand and solve a problem. As an amateur programmer, I am familiar with the difficulty that "you don't know what you don't know" in reference to technical material. Sometimes, you only find out about a handy new technical tool when someone tells you about it. In the same vein, I have had peers inform me of text editor shortcuts, Ruby methods, problem solving approaches, and more subtly, ways of understaning concepts.

Although I've felt positive about most of my pairing sessions, I've had trouble picking out specifically what I liked about my partner's contributions. I recently had the idea to think about this more during the session so that I could provide more specific feedback following our session. This has been a helpful strategy. Providing constructive critical feedback has been difficult for a different reason: I feel very uncomfortable with saying something that might hurt someone's feelings. I try to remind myself of what I know, logically - that what I have to say could help them iprove their skills. And then I do my best to present the feedback honestly and kindly.

I found value in reading my received feedback, and this helped me better understand the value I could provide to others. Much of what I received was positive. The positive feedback helped me identify the techniques and practices I should continue to focus on. Meanwhile the constructive critical feedback helped me identify things I'd like to work on changing. My two most recent pieces pointed out that I am hesitant to speak up. And one partner identified me as a "quiet person." These things were very difficult for me to read, as these are qualities I have been trying to grow out of for my whole life. I have come so far in being articulate and assertive, but not as far as I would like. Still, I appreciate hearing this from others because it reminds me not only that I'm not where I want to be, but also that I can still work to improve in this area. Now, I am making an effort in my sessions to say what I think more confidently, while still respecting my partner's contributions.

Teamwork is an important skill - one that can only be improved with practice and feedback. While the pairing done here at DBC is not always necessary to solve the problem at hand, my peers and I are practicing working productively with others so that we will be able to use these skills later toward larger, more complex goals.
